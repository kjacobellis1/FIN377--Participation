<p><b>Akamai Technologies, Inc.</b> is an American <a href="/wiki/Content_delivery_network" title="Content delivery network">content delivery network</a> (CDN), <a href="/wiki/Cybersecurity" class="mw-redirect" title="Cybersecurity">cybersecurity</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cloud_service" class="mw-redirect" title="Cloud service">cloud service</a> provider headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge, Massachusetts</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. Akamai's <a href="/wiki/Content_delivery_network" title="Content delivery network">content delivery network</a> is one of the world's largest <a href="/wiki/Distributed_computing" title="Distributed computing">distributed computing</a> platforms, responsible for serving between 15% and 30% of all web traffic.<sup id="cite_ref-figures_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-figures-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The company operates a network of servers around the world and rents out capacity on these servers to customers who want their websites to work faster by distributing content from locations close to the user. When a user navigates to the <a href="/wiki/URL" title="URL">URL</a> of an Akamai customer, their browser is redirected to one of Akamai's copies of the website.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akamai_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Akamai Technologies entered the 1998 <a href="/wiki/MIT_$100K_Entrepreneurship_Competition" title="MIT $100K Entrepreneurship Competition">MIT $50K competition</a> with a business proposition based on their research on <a href="/wiki/Consistent_hashing" title="Consistent hashing">consistent hashing</a>, and were selected as one of the finalists.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> By August 1998 they had developed a working prototype, and with the help of Jonathan Seelig and <a href="/wiki/Randall_Kaplan" title="Randall Kaplan">Randall Kaplan</a>, they began taking steps to incorporate the company.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In late 1998 and early 1999, a group of business professionals joined the founding team. Most notably, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Sagan" title="Paul Sagan">Paul Sagan</a>, former president of New Media for <a href="/wiki/Time_Inc." title="Time Inc.">Time Inc.</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_Conrades" title="George Conrades">George Conrades</a>, former chairman and chief executive officer of <a href="/wiki/BBN_Technologies" title="BBN Technologies">BBN Corp.</a> and senior vice president of U.S. operations for <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a>. Conrades became the chief executive officer of Akamai in April 1999 and led the company from start-up to sustained profitability and positive free cash-flow before turning the reins over to Sagan in 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-Fundinguniverse_Akamai_Technologies,_Inc._History_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fundinguniverse_Akamai_Technologies,_Inc._History-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bloomberg_Sagan_2012_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bloomberg_Sagan_2012-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The company launched its commercial service in April 1999 and was listed on the NASDAQ Stock Market from October 29, 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On July 1, 2001, Akamai was added to the <a href="/wiki/Russell_3000_Index" title="Russell 3000 Index">Russell 3000 Index</a> and <a href="/wiki/Russell_2000" class="mw-redirect" title="Russell 2000">Russell 2000</a> Index.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On September 11, 2001, co-founder <a href="/wiki/Daniel_M._Lewin" class="mw-redirect" title="Daniel M. Lewin">Daniel M. Lewin</a> died from stabbing while in flight in the <a href="/wiki/September_11th_attacks" class="mw-redirect" title="September 11th attacks">September 11th attacks</a> at the age of 31. Lewin was aboard <a href="/wiki/American_Airlines_Flight_11" title="American Airlines Flight 11">American Airlines Flight 11</a>, the first plane to crash into the <a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Center_(1973-2001)" class="mw-redirect" title="World Trade Center (1973-2001)">World Trade Center</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2005, Paul Sagan was named chief executive officer of Akamai. Sagan worked to differentiate Akamai from its competitors by expanding the company's breadth of services.<sup id="cite_ref-Bloomberg_Sagan_2012_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bloomberg_Sagan_2012-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Under his leadership the company grew to $1.37 billion in revenues.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Sagan served as chief executive officer until co-founder and current CEO, Tom Leighton, was elected to the position in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Akamai's headquarters are in <a href="/wiki/Kendall_Square" title="Kendall Square">Kendall Square</a>. Akamai started out in <a href="/wiki/Technology_Square_(Cambridge,_Massachusetts)" title="Technology Square (Cambridge, Massachusetts)">Technology Square</a> and later expanded to multiple buildings in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Center" title="Cambridge Center">Cambridge Center</a>. They consolidated their offices in a purpose-built building at 145 Broadway in December 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technologies">Technologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akamai_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Technologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Akamai_Intelligent_Platform">Akamai Intelligent Platform</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akamai_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Akamai Intelligent Platform">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Intelligent Platform is a distributed cloud computing platform that operates worldwide. It is a network of over 240,000 servers<sup id="cite_ref-Facts_&amp;_Figures_&#124;_Akamai_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Facts_&amp;_Figures_|_Akamai-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> deployed in more than 120<sup id="cite_ref-Facts_&amp;_Figures_&#124;_Akamai_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Facts_&amp;_Figures_|_Akamai-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> countries. These servers reside in more than 1,500<sup id="cite_ref-Facts_&amp;_Figures_&#124;_Akamai_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Facts_&amp;_Figures_|_Akamai-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> of the world's networks gathering real-time information about traffic, congestion, and trouble spots. Each Akamai server is equipped with proprietary software that uses complex algorithms to process requests from nearby users, and then serve the requested content.<sup id="cite_ref-Nygren_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nygren-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Content_delivery_process">Content delivery process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akamai_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Content delivery process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Akamaiprocess.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Akamaiprocess.png/300px-Akamaiprocess.png" decoding="async" width="300" height="240" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Akamaiprocess.png/450px-Akamaiprocess.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Akamaiprocess.png/600px-Akamaiprocess.png 2x" data-file-width="944" data-file-height="754" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Akamaiprocess.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Akamai <a href="/wiki/Content_delivery_network" title="Content delivery network">content delivery</a> to a user</div></div></div>
<p>The content delivery process begins with a user submitting a request to a <a href="/wiki/Web_browser" title="Web browser">browser</a>. When a user enters a URL, a <a href="/wiki/DNS" class="mw-redirect" title="DNS">DNS</a> request is triggered and an <a href="/wiki/IP_address" title="IP address">IP address</a> is retrieved. With the IP address, the browser can then contact a web server directly for subsequent requests.<sup id="cite_ref-Zakas_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zakas-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In a content delivery network structure, the <a href="/wiki/Domain_name" title="Domain name">domain name</a> of the URL is translated by the mapping system into the IP address of an <a href="/wiki/Edge_server" class="mw-redirect" title="Edge server">edge server</a> to serve the content to the user.<sup id="cite_ref-Nygren_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nygren-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Akamai delivers web content over its Intelligent Platform by transparently <a href="/wiki/Mirror_website" class="mw-redirect" title="Mirror website">mirroring</a> elements such as <a href="/wiki/HTML" title="HTML">HTML</a>, <a href="/wiki/CSS" class="mw-redirect" title="CSS">CSS</a>, software downloads, and <a href="/wiki/Digital_media" title="Digital media">media</a> objects from customers' servers. The Akamai server is automatically picked depending on the type of content and the user's network location. Receiving content from an Akamai server close to the user allows for faster download times and less vulnerability to <a href="/wiki/Network_congestion" title="Network congestion">network congestion</a>. Akamai claims to provide better scalability by delivering the content over the last-mile from servers close to end-users, avoiding the <a href="/wiki/Middle_mile" title="Middle mile">middle-mile</a> bottleneck of the Internet.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The Download Delivery product line includes HTTP downloads for large downloadable objects, a customizable application for consumers, and analytics tools with metrics that monitor and report on the download process.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Peer-to-peer_networking">Peer-to-peer networking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akamai_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Peer-to-peer networking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In addition to using Akamai's own servers, Akamai delivers content from other end-users' computers, in a form of <a href="/wiki/Peer-to-peer" title="Peer-to-peer">peer-to-peer</a> networking.<sup id="cite_ref-watch_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-watch-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-netsession_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-netsession-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> When users request a download of some large files served by this system, it prompts them to download and install "Akamai NetSession Interface," a download manager used to reduce download time and to increase quality.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> However, this software operates not merely as a download manager (delivering content from the Internet to the user's computer) but also as a peer-to-peer server, delivering content cached on the user's computer to other users' computers.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Network_Operations_Command_Center">Network Operations Command Center</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akamai_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Network Operations Command Center">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Akamai's Network Operations Command Center (NOCC) is used for proactive monitoring and troubleshooting of all servers in the global Akamai network.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The NOCC provides real time statistics of Akamai's web traffic. The traffic metrics update automatically and provide a view of the Internet traffic conditions on Akamai's servers and customer websites.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="State_of_the_Internet">State of the Internet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akamai_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: State of the Internet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The State of the Internet report is a quarterly report Akamai releases based on data gathered from its Intelligent Platform, which provides global Internet statistics such as <a href="/wiki/Internet_Access" class="mw-redirect" title="Internet Access">connection speed</a>, broadband adoption, <a href="/wiki/Attack_(computing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Attack (computing)">attack traffic</a>, network connectivity, and <a href="/wiki/Mobile_device" title="Mobile device">mobile</a> connectivity.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Visualizing_the_Internet">Visualizing the Internet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akamai_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Visualizing the Internet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Akamai's <a href="/wiki/Data_visualization" title="Data visualization">data visualization</a> tools display how data is moving across the Internet in real-time. Viewers are able to see global web conditions, malicious attack traffic, and Internet connectivity.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>  In addition, the net usage indices monitor global news consumption, industry specific traffic, and mobile trends.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>  Akamai also offers the Internet Visualization <a href="/wiki/Mobile_application" class="mw-redirect" title="Mobile application">application</a>, which allows users to view real-time data on their mobile device.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="OPEN_Initiative">OPEN Initiative</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akamai_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: OPEN Initiative">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Litigation">Litigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akamai_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Litigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One of Akamai's patents covers a method of delivering electronic data using a content delivery network. Internet Web site proprietors (content providers) contract with Akamai to deliver their Web sites' content to individual Internet users. The patented method permits large files, such as video or music files, to be stored on Akamai's servers and accessed from those servers by Internet users. This increases the speed with which Internet users access the content from Web sites.
</p><p>Unfortunately for Akamai, its patent was written in a way that called for or permitted actions by multiple persons or entities—such as the content provider customer and the company providing the CDN service.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> Akamai's competitor <a href="/wiki/Limelight_Networks" title="Limelight Networks">Limelight</a> chose to operate its allegedly infringing service in that manner—it performed most steps of the patented process and its customers performed a so-called tagging step. Under the interpretation of patent law at the time when Akamai decided to sue Limelight for patent infringement, a method patent could be held infringed only when a single actor performed all of the steps. The court therefore overturned a $40 million jury verdict in Akamai's favor.
</p><p>Akamai initially lost the case, even taking it to the Supreme Court. The Supreme Court returned the case to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_Federal_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit">United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit</a>, however, with an invitation to re-evaluate its rule, if it chose to do so, that all the steps of a method had to be performed by a single actor for there to be infringement. On remand, the Federal Circuit considered the matter <i>en banc</i> (all active judges of the circuit) and modified its rule. It now held that a patent could also be directly infringed if "an alleged infringer conditions participation in an activity or receipt of a benefit upon performance of a step or steps of a patented method and establishes the manner or timing of that performance." On that basis, the Federal Circuit reinstated the $40 million jury verdict. It said that "Akamai presented substantial evidence demonstrating that Limelight conditions its customers' use of its content delivery network upon its customers' performance of" the steps that Limelight does not itself perform. This has been considered a substantial change in patent law. For further discussion of the case, see <i><a href="/wiki/Akamai_Techs.,_Inc._v._Limelight_Networks,_Inc." title="Akamai Techs., Inc. v. Limelight Networks, Inc.">Akamai Techs., Inc. v. Limelight Networks, Inc.</a></i>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akamai_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Security_Agency" title="National Security Agency">National Security Agency</a> and <a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Investigation" title="Federal Bureau of Investigation">Federal Bureau of Investigation</a> have reportedly used <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook's</a> Akamai content delivery network (CDN) to collect information on Facebook users.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup>  This report appears to show intelligence analysts intercepting communications between Facebook and its CDN provider, but does not indicate Akamai as being complicit in this process.
</p><p>According to researchers from the Universities of Cambridge and California-Berkeley, University College London, and International Computer Science Institute-Berkeley, Akamai has been blocking access to web sites for visitors using <a href="/wiki/Tor_(anonymity_network)" title="Tor (anonymity network)">Tor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup>  This feature, known as 'network lists,' is optional for and implemented by customers of Akamai, and is a legitimate feature common to many major IT vendors that provide network security feeds, such as Emerging Threats, which provides such a feed for free.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

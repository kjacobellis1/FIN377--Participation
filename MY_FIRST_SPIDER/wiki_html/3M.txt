<p>The <b>3M Company</b>, formerly known as the <b>Minnesota Mining and Manufacturing Company</b>, is an American <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational</a> <a href="/wiki/Conglomerate_(company)" title="Conglomerate (company)">conglomerate</a> corporation operating in the fields of industry, worker safety, health care, and consumer goods.<sup id="cite_ref-vault_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vault-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The company produces over 60,000 products under several world-renowned brands,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> including <a href="/wiki/Adhesive" title="Adhesive">adhesives</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abrasive" title="Abrasive">abrasives</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lamination" title="Lamination">laminates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Passive_fire_protection" title="Passive fire protection">passive fire protection</a>, <a href="/wiki/Personal_protective_equipment" title="Personal protective equipment">personal protective equipment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Window_film" title="Window film">window films</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paint_protection_film" title="Paint protection film">paint protection films</a>, dental and orthodontic products, electrical &amp; electronic connecting and insulating materials, medical products, car-care products,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Electronic_circuits" class="mw-redirect" title="Electronic circuits">electronic circuits</a>, healthcare software and optical films.<sup id="cite_ref-3MWHO_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3MWHO-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It is based in <a href="/wiki/Maplewood,_Minnesota" title="Maplewood, Minnesota">Maplewood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a>, a suburb of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Paul,_Minnesota" title="Saint Paul, Minnesota">St. Paul</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>3M made $32.8 billion in total sales in 2018 and ranks number 95 in the <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> list of the largest United States corporations by total revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> As of 2018<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=3M&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, the company has approximately 93,500 employees, and has operations in more than 70 countries.<sup id="cite_ref-10-K_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10-K-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=3M&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Five businessmen founded 3M as a mining venture in <a href="/wiki/Two_Harbors,_Minnesota" title="Two Harbors, Minnesota">Two Harbors, Minnesota</a>, in 1902.<sup id="cite_ref-CPS_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPS-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The goal was to mine <a href="/wiki/Corundum" title="Corundum">corundum</a>, but this failed because the mine's mineral holdings were <a href="/wiki/Anorthosite" title="Anorthosite">anorthosite</a>, which had no commercial value.<sup id="cite_ref-CPS_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPS-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Co-founder John Dwan solicited funds in exchange for stock and Edgar Ober and Lucius Ordway took over the company in 1905.<sup id="cite_ref-CPS_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPS-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The company moved to <a href="/wiki/Duluth,_Minnesota" title="Duluth, Minnesota">Duluth</a> and began researching and producing <a href="/wiki/Sandpaper" title="Sandpaper">sandpaper</a> products.<sup id="cite_ref-CPS_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPS-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/William_L._McKnight" title="William L. McKnight">William L. McKnight</a>, later a key executive, joined the company in 1907, and <a href="/wiki/Archibald_Bush" title="Archibald Bush">A.&#160;G. Bush</a> joined in 1909.<sup id="cite_ref-CPS_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPS-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> 3M finally became financially stable in 1916 and was able to pay <a href="/wiki/Dividend" title="Dividend">dividends</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CPS_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPS-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The company moved to <a href="/wiki/Saint_Paul,_Minnesota" title="Saint Paul, Minnesota">St.&#160;Paul</a> in 1910, where it remained for 52 years before outgrowing the campus and moving to its current headquarters at 3M Center in <a href="/wiki/Maplewood,_Minnesota" title="Maplewood, Minnesota">Maplewood</a>, Minnesota in 1962.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:3M_Museum_2013.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/3M_Museum_2013.jpg/220px-3M_Museum_2013.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="159" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/3M_Museum_2013.jpg/330px-3M_Museum_2013.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/3M_Museum_2013.jpg/440px-3M_Museum_2013.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1628" data-file-height="1178" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:3M_Museum_2013.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/John_Dwan_Office_Building" title="John Dwan Office Building">John Dwan Office Building</a>, where 3M was founded, now a museum</div></div></div>
<p>The founders' original plan was to sell the mineral <a href="/wiki/Corundum" title="Corundum">corundum</a> to manufacturers in the East for making grinding wheels. After selling one load, on June 13, 1902, the five went to the Two Harbors office of company secretary John Dwan, which was on the shore of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Superior" title="Lake Superior">Lake Superior</a> and is now part of the 3M National Museum, and signed papers making Minnesota Mining and Manufacturing a corporation. In reality, however, Dwan and his associates were not selling what they thought; they were really selling the worthless mineral <a href="/wiki/Anorthosite" title="Anorthosite">anorthosite</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-MPR_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MPR-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs additional references to reliable sources. (November 2019)">additional citation(s) needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion_and_modern_history">Expansion and modern history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=3M&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Expansion and modern history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1947, 3M began producing <a href="/wiki/Perfluorooctanoic_acid" title="Perfluorooctanoic acid">perfluorooctanoic acid</a> (PFOA) by <a href="/wiki/Electrochemical_fluorination" title="Electrochemical fluorination">electrochemical fluorination</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Prevedouros2006_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prevedouros2006-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1951, <a href="/wiki/DuPont_(1802%E2%80%932017)" title="DuPont (1802–2017)">DuPont</a> started purchasing PFOA from then-Minnesota Mining and Manufacturing Company for use in the manufacturing of teflon, a product that brought DuPont a billion-dollar-a-year profit by the 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_jan_2016_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_jan_2016-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> DuPont referred to PFOA as C8.<sup id="cite_ref-Emmett2006_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emmett2006-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><span id="3M_Pharmaceuticals"></span><span id="Riker_Laboratories"></span>
</p><p>In the late 1950s, 3M produced the first <a href="/wiki/Asthma_inhaler" class="mw-redirect" title="Asthma inhaler">asthma inhaler</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> but the company did not enter the pharmaceutical industry <i>per se</i> until the mid-1960s with the acquisition of Riker Laboratories, moving it from California to Minnesota.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>  3M retained the name Riker Laboratories for the subsidiary until at least 1985.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>  In the mid-1990s, 3M Pharmaceuticals, as the division came to be called, produced the first <a href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbon" title="Chlorofluorocarbon">CFC</a>-free asthma inhaler in response to adoption of the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Protocol" title="Montreal Protocol">Montreal Protocol</a> by the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>  In the 1980s and 1990s, the company spent fifteen years developing a <a href="/wiki/Topical_cream" class="mw-redirect" title="Topical cream">topical cream</a> delivery technology which led in 1997 to health authority approval and marketing of a <a href="/wiki/Symptomatic_treatment" title="Symptomatic treatment">symptomatic treatment</a> for <a href="/wiki/Genital_herpes" title="Genital herpes">genital herpes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Imiquimod" title="Imiquimod">Aldara</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>  After four decades, 3M divested its pharmaceutical unit through three deals in 2006, netting more than <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$2 billion</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>  At the time, 3M Pharmaceuticals comprised about twenty percent of 3M's health care business and employed just over a thousand people.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:First_and_Mill_3Ms_img14.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/First_and_Mill_3Ms_img14.jpg/250px-First_and_Mill_3Ms_img14.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="169" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/First_and_Mill_3Ms_img14.jpg/375px-First_and_Mill_3Ms_img14.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/First_and_Mill_3Ms_img14.jpg/500px-First_and_Mill_3Ms_img14.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1800" data-file-height="1215" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:First_and_Mill_3Ms_img14.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>3M traffic signals installed in <a href="/wiki/Shelton,_Washington" title="Shelton, Washington">Shelton, Washington</a>. <p>3M Mincom was involved in some of the first <a href="/wiki/Digital_audio" title="Digital audio">digital audio</a> recordings of the late 1970s to see commercial release when a prototype machine was brought to the <a href="/wiki/Sound_80" title="Sound 80">Sound 80</a> studios in <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a>. After drawing on the experience of that prototype recorder, 3M later introduced in 1979 a commercially available digital audio recording system called the "3M Digital Audio Mastering System",<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>3M launched "Press 'n Peel" in stores in four cities in 1977, but results were disappointing.<sup id="cite_ref-FT_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FT-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2ndchance_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2ndchance-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> A year later 3M instead issued free samples directly to consumers in <a href="/wiki/Boise,_Idaho" title="Boise, Idaho">Boise, Idaho</a>, with 94 percent of those who tried them indicating they would buy the product.<sup id="cite_ref-FT_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FT-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The product was sold as "<a href="/wiki/Post-it_Note" title="Post-it Note">Post-its</a>" in 1979 when the rollout introduction began,<sup id="cite_ref-nyt10_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt10-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> and was sold across the United States<sup id="cite_ref-nyt10_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt10-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> from April 6, 1980.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The following year they were launched in Canada and Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As of 2012, 3M was one of the 30 companies included in the <a href="/wiki/Dow_Jones_Industrial_Average" title="Dow Jones Industrial Average">Dow Jones Industrial Average</a>, added on August 9, 1976, and was 97 on the 2011 <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> list.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On September 8, 2008, 3M announced an agreement to acquire Meguiar's, a car-care products company that was family-owned for over a century.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On August 30, 2010, 3M announced that they had acquired <a href="/wiki/Cogent_Systems" title="Cogent Systems">Cogent Systems</a> for $943 million.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On October 13, 2010, 3M completed acquisition of Arizant Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-htanna_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-htanna-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> In December 2011, 3M completed the acquisition of the Winterthur Technology Group, a bonded abrasives company.
</p><p>On January 3, 2012, it was announced that the Office and Consumer Products Division of <a href="/wiki/Avery_Dennison" title="Avery Dennison">Avery Dennison</a> was being bought by 3M for $550 million.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> The transaction was canceled by 3M in September 2012 amid antitrust concerns.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 2013, 3M announced that it was selling <a href="/wiki/Scientific_Anglers" title="Scientific Anglers">Scientific Anglers</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ross_Reels&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ross Reels (page does not exist)">Ross Reels</a> to <a href="/wiki/Orvis" title="Orvis">Orvis</a>. Ross Reels had been acquired by 3M in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-Anderson_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2017, it was announced that 3M was purchasing Johnson Control International Plc's safety gear business, Scott Safety, for $2 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2017, 3M had net sales for the year of $31.657 billion, up from $30.109 billion the year before.<sup id="cite_ref-Yahoo_Finance_3M_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yahoo_Finance_3M-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> In 2018, it was reported that the company would pay $850 million to end the Minnesota water pollution case concerning <a href="/wiki/Perfluorinated_compound" title="Perfluorinated compound">perfluorochemicals</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CNN_on_Pollution_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CNN_on_Pollution-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On May 25, 2018, Michael F. Roman was appointed CEO by the board of directors.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> As of August 2018, 3M India Ltd. was the only listed 3M Company subsidiary.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On December 19, 2018, 3M announced it had entered into a definitive agreement to acquire the technology business of M*Modal, for a total enterprise value of $1.0 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In October 2019, 3M completed the purchase of <a href="/wiki/Acelity" title="Acelity">Acelity</a> and its <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_Concepts" title="Kinetic Concepts">KCI</a> subsidiaries worldwide for $6.7 billion, including assumption of debt and other adjustments.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_record">Environmental record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=3M&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Environmental record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Target_Plaza_South.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Target_Plaza_South.jpg/250px-Target_Plaza_South.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="337" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Target_Plaza_South.jpg/375px-Target_Plaza_South.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/91/Target_Plaza_South.jpg 2x" data-file-width="487" data-file-height="657" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Target_Plaza_South.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Target Light System, built by 3M at <a href="/wiki/Target_Corporation" title="Target Corporation">Target</a> headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>3M's Pollution Prevention Pays (3P) program was established in 1975. The program initially focused on pollution reduction at the plant level and was expanded to promote recycling and reduce waste across all divisions in 1989. By the early 1990s, approximately 2,500 3P projects decreased the company's total global pollutant generation by 50 percent and saved 3M $500–600 million by eliminating the production of waste requiring subsequent treatment.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1983, the <a href="/wiki/Oakdale_Dump" title="Oakdale Dump">Oakdale Dump</a> in <a href="/wiki/Oakdale,_Minnesota" title="Oakdale, Minnesota">Oakdale, Minnesota</a>, was listed as an EPA <a href="/wiki/Superfund" title="Superfund">Superfund</a> site after significant <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> <a href="/wiki/Contamination" title="Contamination">contamination</a> by <a href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound" title="Volatile organic compound">VOCs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metals" title="Heavy metals">heavy metals</a> was uncovered.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> The Oakdale Dump was a 3M dumping site utilized through the 1940s and 1950s.
</p><p>During the 1990s and 2000s, 3M reduced releases of toxic pollutants by 99 percent and greenhouse gas emissions by 72 percent. The company earned the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a>'s Energy Star Award each year the honor was presented, as of 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1999, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">U.S. Environmental Protection Agency</a> began investigating <a href="/wiki/Perfluorinated_compound" title="Perfluorinated compound">perfluorinated chemicals</a> after receiving data on the global distribution and toxicity of <a href="/wiki/Perfluorooctanesulfonic_acid" title="Perfluorooctanesulfonic acid">perfluorooctanesulfonic acid</a> (PFOS).<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> 3M, the former primary producer of PFOS from the U.S., announced the phase-out of PFOS, <a href="/wiki/Perfluorooctanoic_acid" title="Perfluorooctanoic acid">perfluorooctanoic acid</a>, and PFOS-related product production in May 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-3M08_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3M08-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Fellner_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fellner-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Perfluorinated compounds produced by 3M were used in non-stick cookware and stain-resistant fabrics. The <a href="/wiki/Cottage_Grove,_Minnesota" title="Cottage Grove, Minnesota">Cottage Grove</a> facility manufactured PFCs from the 1940s to 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-health.state.mn.us_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-health.state.mn.us-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>In response to PFC contamination of the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a> and surrounding area, 3M stated the area will be "cleaned through a combination of <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> pump-out wells and soil sediment excavation". The restoration plan was based on an analysis of the company property and surrounding lands.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> The on-site water treatment facility that handled the plant's post-production water was not capable of removing the PFCs, which were released into the nearby Mississippi River.<sup id="cite_ref-health.state.mn.us_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-health.state.mn.us-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> The clean-up cost estimate was $50 to $56 million, funded from a $147 million environmental reserve set aside in 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2008, 3M created the Renewable Energy Division within 3M's Industrial and Transportation Business to focus on Energy Generation and Energy Management.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In late 2010, the state of Minnesota sued 3M for $5 billion in punitive damages, claiming they released PFCs—classified a toxic chemical by the EPA—into local waterways.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> A settlement for $850 million was reached in February 2018,<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Fellner_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fellner-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Fellner1_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fellner1-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> although in 2019, 3M, along with the <a href="/wiki/Chemours" title="Chemours">Chemours Company</a> and <a href="/wiki/DuPont" title="DuPont">DuPont</a>, appeared before lawmakers to deny responsibility, with company Senior VP of Corporate Affairs Denise Rutherford arguing that the chemicals pose no human health threats at current levels and have no victims.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operating_facilities">Operating facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=3M&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Operating facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a href="/wiki/File:3Mwatertower.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/3Mwatertower.jpg/120px-3Mwatertower.jpg" decoding="async" width="120" height="168" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/3Mwatertower.jpg/180px-3Mwatertower.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/3Mwatertower.jpg/240px-3Mwatertower.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2257" data-file-height="3161" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:3Mwatertower.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>3M facility in St. Paul, Minnesota</div></div></div>
<p>3M's general offices, corporate research laboratories, and some division laboratories in the US are in St. Paul, Minnesota. In the United States, 3M operates 80 manufacturing facilities in 29 <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">states</a>, and 125 manufacturing and converting facilities in 37 countries outside the US (in 2017).<sup id="cite_ref-Annual2017_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Annual2017-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2016, 3M completed a 400,000-square-foot (37,000&#160;m<sup>2</sup>) research-and-development building that cost $150 million on its Maplewood campus. Seven hundred scientists from various divisions occupy the building. They were previously scattered across the campus. 3M hopes concentrating its research and development in this manner will improve collaboration. 3M received $9.6 million in local <a href="/wiki/Tax_increment_financing" title="Tax increment financing">tax increment financing</a> and relief from state sales taxes in order to assist with development of the building.<sup id="cite_ref-DePass_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DePass-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Selected factory detail information:
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Leadership">Leadership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=3M&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Leadership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Board chairs have included: <a href="/wiki/William_L._McKnight" title="William L. McKnight">William L. McKnight</a> (1949–1966),<sup id="cite_ref-Cummings_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cummings-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Money_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Money-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> Bert S. Cross (1966–1970),<sup id="cite_ref-Move_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Move-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Harry_Heltzer" title="Harry Heltzer">Harry Heltzer</a> (1970–1975),<sup id="cite_ref-Martin_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martin-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> Raymond H. Herzog (1975–1980),<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> Lewis W. Lehr (1980–1986), Allen F. Jacobson (1986–1991),<sup id="cite_ref-Schmitt_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schmitt-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> Livio DeSimone (1991–2001),<sup id="cite_ref-Hagerty_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hagerty-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/James_McNerney" title="James McNerney">James McNerney</a> (2001–2005),<sup id="cite_ref-McNerney_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McNerney-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/George_W._Buckley" title="George W. Buckley">George W. Buckley</a> (2005–2012),<sup id="cite_ref-Dash_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dash-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Retire_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Retire-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Inge_Thulin" title="Inge Thulin">Inge Thulin</a> (2012–2018).<sup id="cite_ref-CNBC_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CNBC-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> Thulin continued to serve as executive chairman until current chair Michael F. Roman was appointed in 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>3M's <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">CEOs</a> have included: Cross (1966–1970),<sup id="cite_ref-Tangled_79-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tangled-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> Heltzer (1970–1975),<sup id="cite_ref-Martin_70-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martin-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> Herzog (1975–1979),<sup id="cite_ref-Tangled_79-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tangled-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Shifts_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shifts-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> Lehr (1979–1986),<sup id="cite_ref-Eccher_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eccher-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> Jacobson (1986–1991),<sup id="cite_ref-Schmitt_72-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schmitt-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> DeSimone (1991–2001),<sup id="cite_ref-Hagerty_73-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hagerty-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> McNerney (2001–2005),<sup id="cite_ref-McNerney_74-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McNerney-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> Robert S. Morrison (2005, interim),<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup> Buckley (2005–2012),<sup id="cite_ref-Dash_75-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dash-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Retire_76-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Retire-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> Thulin (2012–2018), and Roman (2018–present).<sup id="cite_ref-CNBC_77-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CNBC-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>3M's <a href="/wiki/President_(corporate_title)" title="President (corporate title)">presidents</a> have included: Edgar B. Ober (1905–1929),<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup> McKnight (1929–1949),<sup id="cite_ref-Money_67-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Money-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Byrne_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Byrne-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> Richard P. Carlton (1949–1953),<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Herbert_P._Buetow" title="Herbert P. Buetow">Herbert P. Buetow</a> (1953–1963),<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> Cross (1963–1966),<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup> Heltzer (1966–1970),<sup id="cite_ref-Move_68-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Move-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> and Herzog (1970–1975).<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup> In the late 1970s, the position was separated into roles for U.S. and international operations. The position overseeing domestic operations was first held by Lehr,<sup id="cite_ref-Shifts_80-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shifts-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> followed by John Pitblado from 1979 to 1981,<sup id="cite_ref-Sloane_89-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sloane-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> then Jacobson from 1984 to 1991.<sup id="cite_ref-Gilpin_90-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gilpin-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> James A. Thwaits led international operations starting in 1979.<sup id="cite_ref-Sloane_89-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sloane-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> Buckley and Thulin were president during 2005–2012,<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup> and 2012–2018, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-CNBC_77-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CNBC-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>

<p><b>The AES Corporation</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> company that generates and distributes <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">electrical power</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  AES is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Arlington,_Virginia" class="mw-redirect" title="Arlington, Virginia">Arlington, Virginia</a>, and is one of the world's leading power companies, generating and distributing electric power in 15 countries<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and employing 10,500 people worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AES_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The company was founded on January 28, 1981, as <i><b>A</b>pplied <b>E</b>nergy <b>S</b>ervices</i><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Roger_Sant&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Roger Sant (page does not exist)">Roger Sant</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dennis_Bakke&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Dennis Bakke (page does not exist)">Dennis Bakke</a>, two appointees of the Federal Energy Administration under president <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a>. The company was initially a consulting firm; it became AES Corporation, which went public in 1991. Sant was chairman, CEO, and president and Bakke was executive vice president until assuming the position of president in 1987. Bakke would later become the company's CEO in 1994, serving for 8 years until his resignation in 2002, in the midst of a liquidity crisis that followed the collapse of the energy giant <a href="/wiki/Enron" title="Enron">Enron</a>. Sant remained as executive chairman until 2003 and as a member of the board until 2006. Paul Hanrahan was appointed President and CEO and served for 10 years, overseeing the stabilization of the company. In 2012, Hanrahan resigned, his position as President and CEO of the company succeeded by Andres Gluski. AS CEO, Gluski has implemented a strategy of reducing the number of countries in which AES does business in, from 28 to 16, for the purpose of consolidating operations and reducing their costs. Additionally, he also began a program of reducing the company's total carbon emission intensity.
</p><p>Bakke and Sant oversaw much of AES's initial global expansion, building power plants in 29 countries and expanding its staff from 1,400 to 32,000 employees, and also instilled a system of decentralized management that emphasized social responsibility above profit.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In recent years, AES has also signaled a commitment to providing their consumers and clients with renewable forms of energy, and their operations across the world have increasingly focused on the construction, and provision of solar and wind based energy storage systems.
</p><p>AES acquired the assets of <a href="/wiki/Indianapolis_Power_%26_Light" title="Indianapolis Power &amp; Light">Indianapolis Power &amp; Light</a>, Ipalco, in 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>AES acquired the Chilean-based subsidiary <i>Gener</i> in 2000. 
AES acquired <a href="/wiki/DPL_Inc." title="DPL Inc.">DPL Inc.</a> known as Dayton Power &amp; Light in 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In fiscal 2015 AES's total revenue was $15 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-AES-Corporation-Feb-2015-10-K_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AES-Corporation-Feb-2015-10-K-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2018, AES acquired the subsidiary <i>sPower</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_projects">Major projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AES_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Major projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fluence">Fluence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AES_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fluence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Launched during January 2018, <i>Fluence</i> is a joint venture between <i>AES Energy Storage</i> and <a href="/wiki/Siemens" title="Siemens">Siemens</a> that is focused on the development of, and expansion of energy storage technologies and services. Chaired by former AES vice-president for energy storage platforms Stephen Coughlin, and headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Washington_D.C." class="mw-redirect" title="Washington D.C.">Washington D.C.</a>, Fluence aims to implement AES's extensive research into the potential of lithium-ion powered energy sources by relying upon Siemens' expansive global presence in the industrial sector; for the purpose of addressing the rapidly rising demand for clean energy technologies.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Fluence has been deployed in 16 different countries, with major projects including the following:<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>The creation of a new 40 megawatt storage facility on behalf of San Diego Gas &amp; Electric's new 40 MW storage facility.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Three solar powered projects throughout Arizona and two 10 MW projects in the Dominican Republic.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The installation of six storage projects across Germany that will provide grid stabilization.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The construction of a hybrid microgrid on the Mediterranean Island of <a href="/wiki/Ventotene" title="Ventotene">Ventotene</a>. Said microgrid will be developed as per an agreement between Fluence and the Italian energy utility Enel, and will combine diesel generators with renewables in order to aid in the efforts to reduce carbon costs and emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The key technology platforms developed by Fluence include the following.</li></ol>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Key_Fluence_technologies">Key Fluence technologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AES_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Key Fluence technologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Fluence is expected to employ three different types of grid technology.
</p><p>i):<i>SIESTORAGE</i>: An electrical energy storage system fueled predominantly by wind and solar energy. Siestorage relies upon the closed-loop controls and pulse modulation built into its semiconductors, in order to provide consumers with increased dependability.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>ii):<i>Advancion</i>: A storage system that is made up of several small, modular nodes and powered by Lithium ion batteries, which enables the Advancion energy store system to provide consumers with a heightened degree of consistency in performance.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>iii): <i>Sunflux Energy</i>: Announced in January 2018, Sunflex was developed for the purpose of expanding upon the possibilities offered by photovoltaic solar energy. This technology is built to capture energy during peak solar hours in order to expand energy delivery.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Based_in_Vietnam">Based in Vietnam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AES_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Based in Vietnam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<ul><li><i><b>Mong Duong 2</b></i>: Located in [Cam Pha], this 1,240 MW coal fired plant became commercially operational in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> This plant, constructed under a Build-Operate Transfer (BOT) agreement between AES and state owned entities Electricity of Vietnam (EVN) and Vinacomin, is the largest power project in the nation's private sector.</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties_and_subsidiaries_in_India">Properties and subsidiaries in India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AES_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Properties and subsidiaries in India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul><li><i><b>OPGC Plant</b></i>: Acquired by AES in 1998, this coal power plant has a total capacity of 420 MW. It is located in <a href="/wiki/Odisha" title="Odisha">Odisha</a>.</li>
<li><i><b>OPGC II</b></i>: Currently under construction, this coal powered plant is expected to boast 1,300 MW capacity.</li></ul>

<p><b>Activision Blizzard, Inc.</b> is an American video game <a href="/wiki/Holding_company" title="Holding company">holding company</a> based in <a href="/wiki/Santa_Monica,_California" title="Santa Monica, California">Santa Monica, California</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The company was founded in July 2008 through the merger of Activision, Inc. (the publicly traded parent company of <a href="/wiki/Activision_Publishing" class="mw-redirect" title="Activision Publishing">Activision Publishing</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Vivendi_Games" title="Vivendi Games">Vivendi Games</a>. The company is traded on the <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ" class="mw-redirect" title="NASDAQ">NASDAQ</a> stock exchange under the ticker symbol ATVI,<sup id="cite_ref-ukgamespotabrendan_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ukgamespotabrendan-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and since 2015 has been one of the stocks that make up the <a href="/wiki/S%26P_500_Index" title="S&amp;P 500 Index">S&amp;P 500</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-actsp500_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-actsp500-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Activision Blizzard currently includes five business units:<sup id="cite_ref-ourcompany_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ourcompany-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Activision Publishing, <a href="/wiki/Blizzard_Entertainment" title="Blizzard Entertainment">Blizzard Entertainment</a>, <a href="/wiki/King_(company)" title="King (company)">King</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-nyt-activisionking_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-activisionking-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Gaming" title="Major League Gaming">Major League Gaming</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-advistionbeefsup_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-advistionbeefsup-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Activision_Blizzard_Studios" title="Activision Blizzard Studios">Activision Blizzard Studios</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-fortuneesportsa_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fortuneesportsa-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-goldfrbskylander_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-goldfrbskylander-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The company owns and operates additional studios under an independent studios model under Activision Publishing, including <a href="/wiki/Treyarch" title="Treyarch">Treyarch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Infinity_Ward" title="Infinity Ward">Infinity Ward</a>, <a href="/wiki/High_Moon_Studios" title="High Moon Studios">High Moon Studios</a> and <a href="/wiki/Toys_for_Bob" title="Toys for Bob">Toys for Bob</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ourcompany_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ourcompany-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Among major intellectual properties produced by Activision-Blizzard includes <i><a href="/wiki/Call_of_Duty" title="Call of Duty">Call of Duty</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Guitar_Hero" title="Guitar Hero">Guitar Hero</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Tony_Hawk%27s" class="mw-redirect" title="Tony Hawk&#39;s">Tony Hawk's</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Spyro" title="Spyro">Spyro</a></i>/<i><a href="/wiki/Skylanders" title="Skylanders">Skylanders</a></i> through Activision's studios,  <i><a href="/wiki/World_of_Warcraft" title="World of Warcraft">World of Warcraft</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/StarCraft" title="StarCraft">StarCraft</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Diablo_(series)" title="Diablo (series)">Diablo</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Hearthstone_(video_game)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hearthstone (video game)">Hearthstone</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Heroes_of_the_Storm" title="Heroes of the Storm">Heroes of the Storm</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Overwatch_(video_game)" title="Overwatch (video game)">Overwatch</a></i> through Blizzard Entertainment, and mobile titles including <i><a href="/wiki/Candy_Crush_Saga" title="Candy Crush Saga">Candy Crush Saga</a></i> through King. Activision-Blizzard's titles have broken a number of release records.<sup id="cite_ref-next-genbiz_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-next-genbiz-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-polygonselling_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-polygonselling-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-techspotbest_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-techspotbest-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> As of March&#160;2018<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Activision_Blizzard&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, it is the largest game company in the Americas and Europe in terms of revenue and market capitalization.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Activision_Blizzard.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Activision_Blizzard.svg/250px-Activision_Blizzard.svg.png" decoding="async" width="250" height="175" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Activision_Blizzard.svg/375px-Activision_Blizzard.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Activision_Blizzard.svg/500px-Activision_Blizzard.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="280" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Activision_Blizzard.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Former logo used until 2011.</div></div></div>
<h3><span id="Background_and_formation_.282007.E2.80.932008.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_formation_(2007–2008)">Background and formation (2007–2008)</span></h3>
<p>In 1991, <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Kotick" title="Bobby Kotick">Bobby Kotick</a> and a group of investors had acquired the failing Mediagenic, the company that Activision had become from former leadership. Kotick instituted a large restructuring to pull the company out of debt, including renaming the company as Activision and moving it to Santa Monica, California. After bringing the company back to profitability by 1997, Kotick spent the next decade expanding Activision's products through acquisitions of an estimated 25 studios, which resulted in several successful series of games, including <i><a href="/wiki/Tony_Hawk%27s_(series)" title="Tony Hawk&#39;s (series)">Tony Hawk's</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Call_of_Duty" title="Call of Duty">Call of Duty</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Guitar_Hero" title="Guitar Hero">Guitar Hero</a></i>. However, by around 2006, the popularity of <a href="/wiki/Massively_multiplayer_online" class="mw-redirect" title="Massively multiplayer online">massively multiplayer online</a> (MMO) games started to grow. Such games provide a constant revenue stream to their publishers, rather than only a single purchase, making them a more valuable proposition. None of Activision's subsidiaries had an MMO or the capability to make one quickly. Furthering this, Activision was facing tougher competition from companies like <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Arts" title="Electronic Arts">Electronic Arts</a>, as well as slowdowns in sales of their key game series.<sup id="cite_ref-forbes_kotick_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbes_kotick-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Around 2006, Kotick reached out to <a href="/wiki/Jean-Bernard_L%C3%A9vy" title="Jean-Bernard Lévy">Jean-Bernard Lévy</a>, the CEO of the French media conglomerate <a href="/wiki/Vivendi" title="Vivendi">Vivendi</a>. Vivendi at that time had a small games division, <a href="/wiki/Vivendi_Games" title="Vivendi Games">Vivendi Games</a>, a holding company principally for <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Entertainment" title="Sierra Entertainment">Sierra Entertainment</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blizzard_Entertainment" title="Blizzard Entertainment">Blizzard Entertainment</a>. Kotick wanted to get access to Blizzard's <i><a href="/wiki/World_of_Warcraft" title="World of Warcraft">World of Warcraft</a></i>, a successful MMO, and suggested a means to acquire this to Lévy. Lévy instead offered that he would be willing to merge Vivendi Games with Activision, but only if Vivendi kept majority control of the merged company. According to those close to Kotick, Kotick was concerned about this offer as it would force him to cede control of Activision. However, after talking to Blizzard's CEO <a href="/wiki/Mike_Morhaime" class="mw-redirect" title="Mike Morhaime">Mike Morhaime</a>, Kotick recognized that Vivendi would be able to give them inroads into the growing video game market in China.<sup id="cite_ref-forbes_kotick_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbes_kotick-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Kotick proposed the merger to Activision's board, which agreed to it in December 2007. The new company was to be named Activision Blizzard, and would retain its central headquarters in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>. <a href="/wiki/Robert_Kotick" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert Kotick">Bobby Kotick</a> of Activision was announced as the new president and <a href="/wiki/CEO" class="mw-redirect" title="CEO">CEO</a>, while René Penisson of Vivendi was appointed <a href="/wiki/Chairman" class="mw-redirect" title="Chairman">chairman</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a> permitted the merger to take place in April 2008, approving that there weren't any antitrust issues in the merger deal.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> On July 8, 2008, Activision announced that stockholders had agreed to merge, and the deal closed the next day for an estimated transaction amount of US$18.9 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-Merger_Approved_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Merger_Approved-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Vivendi was the majority shareholder, with a 52% stake in the company.<sup id="cite_ref-Merger_Approved_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Merger_Approved-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The rest of the shares were held by institutional and private investors, and were to be left open for trading on the <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ" class="mw-redirect" title="NASDAQ">NASDAQ</a> stock market for a time under <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ" class="mw-redirect" title="NASDAQ">NASDAQ</a>:&#160;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.nasdaq.com/symbol/atvid">ATVID</a>, and subsequently as <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ" class="mw-redirect" title="NASDAQ">NASDAQ</a>:&#160;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.nasdaq.com/symbol/atvi">ATVI</a> (Activision's stock ticker). At this point, Lévy replaced René Penisson as chairman of Activision Blizzard.<sup id="cite_ref-ukgamespotabrendan_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ukgamespotabrendan-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> While Blizzard retained its autonomy and corporate leadership in the merger, other Vivendi Games divisions such as <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Entertainment" title="Sierra Entertainment">Sierra</a> ceased operation.<sup id="cite_ref-mergersierranotso_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mergersierranotso-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> With the merger, Kotick was quoted stating if a Sierra product did not meet Activision's requirements, they "won't likely be retained."<sup id="cite_ref-mergersierranotso_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mergersierranotso-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Some of these games ultimately were published by other studios, including <i><a href="/wiki/Ghostbusters:_The_Video_Game" title="Ghostbusters: The Video Game">Ghostbusters: The Video Game</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Br%C3%BCtal_Legend" title="Brütal Legend">Brütal Legend</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chronicles_of_Riddick:_Assault_on_Dark_Athena" title="The Chronicles of Riddick: Assault on Dark Athena">The Chronicles of Riddick: Assault on Dark Athena</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/50_Cent:_Blood_on_the_Sand" title="50 Cent: Blood on the Sand">50 Cent: Blood on the Sand</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> However, a number of Sierra's games such as <i><a href="/wiki/Crash_Bandicoot" title="Crash Bandicoot">Crash Bandicoot</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Spyro" title="Spyro">Spyro the Dragon</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Prototype_(video_game)" title="Prototype (video game)">Prototype</a></i> were retained and are now published by Activision.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="New_titles_and_sales_records_.282009.E2.80.932012.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="New_titles_and_sales_records_(2009–2012)">New titles and sales records (2009–2012)</span></h3>
<p>Activision Blizzard does not publish games under its central name and instead uses the Activision Publishing subsidiary and its studios to publish games.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In early 2010, the independent studio <a href="/wiki/Bungie" title="Bungie">Bungie</a> entered into a 10-year publishing agreement with Activision Blizzard.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> By the end of 2010, Activision Blizzard was the largest video games publisher in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>  The 2011 release of Activision Blizzard's <i><a href="/wiki/Call_of_Duty:_Modern_Warfare_3" title="Call of Duty: Modern Warfare 3">Call of Duty: Modern Warfare 3</a></i> grossed $400 million in the US and UK alone in its first 24 hours, making it the biggest entertainment launch of all time.<sup id="cite_ref-MW3_US_And_UK_Sales_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MW3_US_And_UK_Sales-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> It was also the third consecutive year the <i><a href="/wiki/Call_of_Duty" title="Call of Duty">Call of Duty</a></i> series broke the biggest launch record; 2010's <i><a href="/wiki/Call_of_Duty:_Black_Ops" title="Call of Duty: Black Ops">Call of Duty: Black Ops</a></i> grossed $360 million on day one; and 2009's <i><a href="/wiki/Call_of_Duty:_Modern_Warfare_2" title="Call of Duty: Modern Warfare 2">Call of Duty: Modern Warfare 2</a></i> brought in $310 million.<sup id="cite_ref-next-genbiz_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-next-genbiz-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Call_of_Duty:_Black_Ops_III" title="Call of Duty: Black Ops III">Call of Duty: Black Ops III</a></i> grossed $550 million in worldwide sales during its opening weekend in 2015, making it the biggest entertainment launch of the year.<sup id="cite_ref-polygonselling_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-polygonselling-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-techspotbest_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-techspotbest-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2011, Activision Blizzard debuted its <i><a href="/wiki/Skylanders" title="Skylanders">Skylanders</a></i> franchise,<sup id="cite_ref-BKiSpyro_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BKiSpyro-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> which led to the press crediting the company with inventing and popularizing a new <a href="/wiki/Toys-to-life" title="Toys-to-life">toys-to-life</a> category.<sup id="cite_ref-BKiSpyro_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BKiSpyro-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The first release <i><a href="/wiki/Skylanders:_Spyro%27s_Adventure" title="Skylanders: Spyro&#39;s Adventure">Skylanders: Spyro's Adventure</a></i> was nominated for two <a href="/wiki/Toy_Industry_Association" class="mw-redirect" title="Toy Industry Association">Toy Industry Association</a> awards in 2011: "Game of the Year" and "Innovative Toy of the Year".<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> <i>Skylanders: Spyro's Adventure</i> and its sequels were released for major consoles and PC, and many were released on mobile devices as well.<sup id="cite_ref-BKiSpyro_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BKiSpyro-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Split_from_Vivendi_and_growth_.282013.E2.80.932014.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Split_from_Vivendi_and_growth_(2013–2014)">Split from Vivendi and growth (2013–2014)</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Activision-Gamescom_2013.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Activision-Gamescom_2013.JPG/230px-Activision-Gamescom_2013.JPG" decoding="async" width="230" height="173" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Activision-Gamescom_2013.JPG/345px-Activision-Gamescom_2013.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Activision-Gamescom_2013.JPG/460px-Activision-Gamescom_2013.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1944" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Activision-Gamescom_2013.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Activision Blizzard at <a href="/wiki/Gamescom#Gamescom_2013" title="Gamescom">Gamescom 2013</a>, where the company exhibited 2013 titles such as <i><a href="/wiki/Call_of_Duty:_Ghosts" title="Call of Duty: Ghosts">Call of Duty: Ghosts</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Skylanders:_Swap_Force" title="Skylanders: Swap Force">Skylanders: Swap Force</a></i>.</div></div></div>
<p>On July 25, 2013, Activision Blizzard announced the purchase of 429 million shares from owner Vivendi for $5.83 billion, dropping the shareholder from a 63% stake to 11.8% by the end of the deal in September.<sup id="cite_ref-transofmrativepurchase_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-transofmrativepurchase-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> At the conclusion of the deal, Vivendi was no longer Activision Blizzard's parent company,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> and Activision Blizzard became an independent company as a majority of the shares became owned by the public. Bobby Kotick and Brian Kelly retained a 24.4% stake in the company overall. In addition, Kotick remained the president and CEO, with Brian Kelly taking over as <a href="/wiki/Chairman" class="mw-redirect" title="Chairman">chairman</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-transofmrativepurchase_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-transofmrativepurchase-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> On October 12, 2013, shortly after approval from the <a href="/wiki/Delaware_Supreme_Court" title="Delaware Supreme Court">Delaware Supreme Court</a>, the company completed the buyback, along the lines of the original plan.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Vivendi sold half its remaining stake on May 22, 2014, reducing its ownership to 5.8%.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> and completely exited two years later.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Activision Blizzard released a new title, <i><a href="/wiki/Destiny_(video_game)" title="Destiny (video game)">Destiny</a></i>, on September 9, 2014. The game made over $500 million in retail sales on the first day of release, setting a record for the biggest first day launch of a new gaming franchise.<sup id="cite_ref-BKaforbesonff_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BKaforbesonff-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> On November 5, 2013, the company released <i><a href="/wiki/Call_of_Duty:_Ghosts" title="Call of Duty: Ghosts">Call of Duty: Ghosts</a></i>, which was written by screenwriter <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Gaghan" title="Stephen Gaghan">Stephen Gaghan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>  On its first release day the game sold $1 billion into retail.<sup id="cite_ref-BKaforbesonff_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BKaforbesonff-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> In 2014, Activision Blizzard was the fifth largest <a href="/wiki/Lists_of_video_game_companies" title="Lists of video game companies">gaming company</a> by revenue worldwide,<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> with total assets of US$14.746 billion and total equity estimated at US$7.513 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="S.26P_500_and_new_divisions_.282015.E2.80.93present.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="S&amp;P_500_and_new_divisions_(2015–present)">S&amp;P 500 and new divisions (2015–present)</span></h3>
<p>Activision Blizzard joined the <a href="/wiki/S%26P_500_Index" title="S&amp;P 500 Index">S&amp;P 500</a> on August 28, 2015, becoming one of only two companies on the list related to gaming, alongside <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Arts" title="Electronic Arts">Electronic Arts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-actsp500_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-actsp500-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The company released the next iteration of the <i>Skylanders</i> franchise in September 2015, which added vehicles to the "toys to life" category.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> On September 15, 2015, Activision and <a href="/wiki/Bungie" title="Bungie">Bungie</a> released <i><a href="/wiki/Destiny:_The_Taken_King" title="Destiny: The Taken King">Destiny: The Taken King</a></i>, the follow up to the <i><a href="/wiki/Destiny_(video_game)" title="Destiny (video game)">Destiny</a></i> saga. Two days later, <a href="/wiki/Sony" title="Sony">Sony</a> announced that the game broke the record for the most downloaded day-one game in <a href="/wiki/PlayStation" title="PlayStation">PlayStation</a> history, in terms of both total players and peak online concurrency.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Activision Blizzard  acquired social gaming company <a href="/wiki/King_(company)" title="King (company)">King</a>, creator of casual game <i><a href="/wiki/Candy_Crush_Saga" title="Candy Crush Saga">Candy Crush Saga</a></i>, for $5.9 billion in November 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt-activisionking_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-activisionking-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In November 2015, Activision Blizzard announced the formation of <a href="/wiki/Activision_Blizzard_Studios" title="Activision Blizzard Studios">Activision Blizzard Studios</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Film_production" class="mw-redirect" title="Film production">film production</a> arm that would produce films and television series based on Activision Blizzard's franchises.<sup id="cite_ref-goldfrbskylander_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-goldfrbskylander-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The outfit is co-headed by producer <a href="/wiki/Stacey_Sher" title="Stacey Sher">Stacey Sher</a> and former <a href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">The Walt Disney Company</a> executive Nick van Dyk.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2017, Activision Blizzard joined the <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> becoming the third gaming company in history to make the list after <a href="/wiki/Atari" title="Atari">Atari</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Arts" title="Electronic Arts">Electronic Arts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In its 2018 fiscal year earnings call to shareholders in February 2019, Kotick stated that while the company had seen a record year in revenue, they would be laying off around 775 people or around 8% of their workforce in non-management divisions, "de-prioritizing initiatives that are not meeting expectations and reducing certain non-development and administrative-related costs across the business", according to Kotick.<sup id="cite_ref-arstech_feb2019_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arstech_feb2019-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Kotick stated that they plan to put more resources towards their development teams and focus on esports, Battle.net services, and the publisher's core games which include <i>Candy Crush</i>, <i>Call of Duty</i>, <i>Overwatch</i>, <i>Warcraft</i>, <i>Diablo</i>, and <i>Hearthstone</i>. Prior to this, Activision Blizzard and Bungie agreed to terminate their distribution deal with <i>Destiny 2</i> as it was not bringing in expected revenue for Activision, with Bungie otherwise retaining all rights to <i>Destiny</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-arstech_feb2019_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arstech_feb2019-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> This transaction allowed Activision-Blizzard to report <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$164 million</span> as part of its 2018 fiscal year filings.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_structure">Corporate structure</span></h2>
<p>As of February 2018, Activision Blizzard is divided into three key business segments:<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Activision" title="Activision">Activision Publishing</a>, which handles the development, production, and distribution of video games from its subsidiary studios.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Blizzard_Entertainment" title="Blizzard Entertainment">Blizzard Entertainment</a>, which handles the development, production, and distribution of Blizzard's games. It also houses the company's esports activities including MLG and the Overwatch League.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/King_(company)" title="King (company)">King</a>, which handles the development and distribution of its mobile games.</li></ul>
<p>There are also two non-reporting segments within Activition Blizzard. <a href="/wiki/Activision_Blizzard_Studios" title="Activision Blizzard Studios">Activision Blizzard Studios</a> oversees the production of film and television entertainment based on the company's properties. Activision Blizzard Distribution provides logistical support for Activision Blizzard's distribution within Europe.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Esports_initiatives">Esports initiatives</span></h2>
<p>Activision Blizzard owns the <i><a href="/wiki/Call_of_Duty" title="Call of Duty">Call of Duty</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/StarCraft" title="StarCraft">StarCraft</a></i> franchises, both of which have been popular as <a href="/wiki/Esports" title="Esports">esports</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-advistionbeefsup_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-advistionbeefsup-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ars-mlgshuttered_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ars-mlgshuttered-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> On October 21, 2015, Activision Blizzard announced the upcoming establishment of a new e-sports division.<sup id="cite_ref-fortuneesportsa_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fortuneesportsa-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Named Activision Blizzard Media Networks, the division is led by sports executive <a href="/wiki/Steve_Bornstein" title="Steve Bornstein">Steve Bornstein</a> and <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Gaming" title="Major League Gaming">Major League Gaming</a> (MLG) co-founder <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Gaming" title="Major League Gaming">Mike Sepso</a>, with assets from the acquisition of the now defunct <a href="/wiki/IGN#IGN_Pro_League" title="IGN">IGN Pro League</a>. Bornstein was appointed the new division's chairman.<sup id="cite_ref-advistionbeefsup_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-advistionbeefsup-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ars-mlgshuttered_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ars-mlgshuttered-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> On December 31, 2015, it was reported that "substantially all" of Major League Gaming's assets would be acquired by Activision Blizzard.<sup id="cite_ref-advistionbeefsup_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-advistionbeefsup-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ars-mlgshuttered_50-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ars-mlgshuttered-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> <i>The New York Times</i> reported that the acquisition was intended to bolster Activision Blizzard's push into e-sports, as well as its plan to develop an e-sports <a href="/wiki/Cable_channel" class="mw-redirect" title="Cable channel">cable channel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt-mlgbuy_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-mlgbuy-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Reports indicated that MLG would be shuttered, and that the majority of the purchase price would go towards paying off the company's debt.<sup id="cite_ref-advistionbeefsup_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-advistionbeefsup-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ars-mlgshuttered_50-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ars-mlgshuttered-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> Activision Blizzard acquired MLG on January 4, 2016<sup id="cite_ref-nyt-mlgbuy_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-mlgbuy-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> for $46 million.<sup id="cite_ref-advistionbeefsup_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-advistionbeefsup-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ars-mlgshuttered_50-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ars-mlgshuttered-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In November 2016, Blizzard Entertainment, a subsidiary of Activision Blizzard, announced the launch of <a href="/wiki/Overwatch_League" title="Overwatch League">Overwatch League</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>  a professional video gaming league. The league's first season began during the second half of 2017 with 12 teams.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> The league's structure is based on traditional sports structures, including recruiting traditional sports executives as team owners, such as <a href="/wiki/Robert_Kraft" title="Robert Kraft">Robert Kraft</a>, owner of the <a href="/wiki/New_England_Patriots" title="New England Patriots">New England Patriots</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Wilpon" title="Jeff Wilpon">Jeff Wilpon</a>, COO of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mets" title="New York Mets">New York Mets</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The inaugural Overwatch Grand Finals was played at the <a href="/wiki/Barclays_Center" title="Barclays Center">Barclays Center</a> in Brooklyn in July 2018 and attracted 10.8 million viewers worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> The league hopes to have 18 teams competing during the second season in 2019, with an ultimate goal of 28 teams across the world.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2018, Activision Blizzard signed a multi-year deal with Walt Disney to stream Overwatch League games on both <a href="/wiki/ESPN" title="ESPN">ESPN</a> and <a href="/wiki/Disney_XD" title="Disney XD">Disney XD</a> cable channels.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> The company also secured an exclusive multiyear deal with <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> to stream all subsequent Activision Blizzard espots events, including <i>Call of Duty</i> and <i>Overwatch</i> events, through <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a>, and to use Google's cloud services for its game hosting infrastructure; this came after a prior two-year deal with <a href="/wiki/Twitch_(service)" title="Twitch (service)">Twitch</a> for the Overwatch League had concluded. The deal with YouTube was estimated to be valued at <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$160 million</span>, double what it had with Twitch.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Call_of_Duty_Endowment">Call of Duty Endowment</span></h2>
<p>Since 2009, when CEO Kotick launched Call of Duty Endowment (CODE), over 50,000 veterans have been placed in high-quality jobs.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> In 2013 CODE started the "Seal of Distinction" program which recognizes non-profit organizations that are successful placing veterans in good jobs. Winners receive a $30,000 grant to use in their veteran job placement activities.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> The goal of CODE is to help 100,000 US and UK veterans find high-quality jobs by 2024.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal">Legal</span></h2>
<h3><span id="Worlds.2C_Inc."></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Worlds,_Inc.">Worlds, Inc.</span></h3>
<p>Worlds, Inc. was issued several United States patents around 2009 related to "System and method for enabling users to interact in a virtual space", which generally described a method of server/client communications for multiplayer video games, where players would communicate through <a href="/wiki/Avatar" title="Avatar">avatars</a>. In early 2009, Worlds, Inc. stated its intent to challenge publishers and developers of MMOs, naming Activision as one of its intended targets.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> Worlds, Inc. had already challenged <a href="/wiki/NCSoft" title="NCSoft">NCSoft</a> for its MMOs in 2008. The companies ultimately settled out of court by 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Worlds, Inc. launched its formal lawsuit against Activision Blizzard, including both Blizzard Entertainment and Activision Publishing, in March 2012, stating that <i>Call of Duty</i> and <i>World of Warcraft</i> infringed on their patents.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bfilessuitcountersuit_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bfilessuitcountersuit-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> Activision Publishing filed a separate patent infringement lawsuit in October 2013, asserting that Worlds, Inc. was using two Activision-owned patents in its <i>Worlds Player</i> software,<sup id="cite_ref-bfilessuitcountersuit_72-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bfilessuitcountersuit-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> but this suit was dismissed with prejudice by June 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Worlds, Inc. case against Activision, the judge issued a summary judgement in Activision's favor, as they had demonstrated that Worlds, Inc. had demonstrated the technologies of their patents in their client programs <i>AlphaWorld</i> and <i>World Chat</i>, released before the 1995 priority date, though this was related to filing irregularities that were subsequently corrected by the Patent Office.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> Activision did not challenge the updated patents through an <a href="/wiki/Inter_partes_review" title="Inter partes review">inter partes review</a> (IPR), and subsequently after a statutory one-year waiting period, Worlds, Inc. filed a subsequently lawsuit against Activision, asserting <i><a href="/wiki/Call_of_Duty:_Ghosts" title="Call of Duty: Ghosts">Call of Duty: Ghosts</a></i> violated their resolved patents.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> Later, Worlds, Inc. stated the intent to add <a href="/wiki/Bungie" title="Bungie">Bungie</a> to the lawsuit contending that <i>Destiny</i> also fell afoul of their patents. Bungie subsequently filed three IPRs with the Patent Office for each of the three Worlds, Inc. patents at the core of the lawsuit.
</p><p>The new Worlds, Inc. case against Activision Blizzard was heard on October 3, 2014. With Bungie's IPRs pending at the Patent Office, the judge put the trial on hold pending the outcome of the IPRs. Worlds, Inc. challenged the IPRs at the Patent Office, as they did not include Activision as an interested party, a requirement that would have been necessary given the publisher/developer relationship between Activision and Bungie. The Patent Office did not accept this argument, and subsequently agreed with the Bungie IPRs that portions of Worlds, Inc. patents were invalid. Worlds, Inc. appealed to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_Federal_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit">Federal Circuit Appeals Court</a>, challenging the validity of the IPRs due to the lack of Activision's involvement. The Federal Circuit court ruled in favor of Worlds, Inc. in September 2018, invalidating the Patent Office's decision.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> Worlds, Inc.'s case presently remains at the Patent Office stage, which is re-reviewing the IPRs in consideration of the Federal Circuit's ruling.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Infinity_Ward">Infinity Ward</span></h3>
<p>In early 2010, Activision fired <a href="/wiki/Vince_Zampella" class="mw-redirect" title="Vince Zampella">Vince Zampella</a> and Jason West, two of the founders of its studio <a href="/wiki/Infinity_Ward" title="Infinity Ward">Infinity Ward</a>, on the basis of "breaches of contract and insubordination"; the move caused several other Infinity Ward staff to resign. Zampella and West created a new studio, <a href="/wiki/Respawn_Entertainment" title="Respawn Entertainment">Respawn Entertainment</a>, with help from <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Arts" title="Electronic Arts">Electronic Arts</a>' partner program, hiring several of those that departed Infinity Ward in their wake.<sup id="cite_ref-IW_v._Activision_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IW_v._Activision-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Veto_power_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Veto_power-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Zampella and West filed a lawsuit in April 2010 against Activision, claiming unpaid royalties on the studio's <i><a href="/wiki/Call_of_Duty:_Modern_Warfare_2" title="Call of Duty: Modern Warfare 2">Call of Duty: Modern Warfare 2</a></i>. Activision filed a countersuit against the two, accusing the pair of being "self-serving schemers".<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup> Activision later sought to add <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Arts" title="Electronic Arts">Electronic Arts</a> to their suit, discovering that Zampella and West had been in discussions with them while still working for Activision, and further added claims against Zampella and West that the two had not returned all material related to <i>Call of Duty</i> while they were working at Respawn. A separate lawsuit was filed against Activision in April 2010 by several current and former members of Infinity Ward on the same basis of lack of unpaid royalties.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>All parties came to an undisclosed settlement to end all suits by May 2012. Electronic Arts and Activision had settled separately on Activision's charges of poaching employees, while the suits between Activision, Zampella, West, and the Infinity Ward employee group were settled by the end of May 2012. All settlements were made for undisclosed amounts.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

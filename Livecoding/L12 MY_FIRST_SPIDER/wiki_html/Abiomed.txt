<p><b>Abiomed</b> is a manufacturer of <a href="/wiki/Medical_implant" class="mw-redirect" title="Medical implant">medical implant</a> devices, including the <a href="/wiki/AbioCor" title="AbioCor">AbioCor</a> <a href="/wiki/Artificial_heart" title="Artificial heart">artificial heart</a> and <a href="/wiki/Impella" title="Impella">Impella</a>. It is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Danvers,_Massachusetts" title="Danvers, Massachusetts">Danvers, Massachusetts</a> and has three additional offices, two in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> in the cities of Berlin and Aachen, and another in Japan. Michael R. Minogue joined Abiomed in 2004, and serves as CEO, Chairman and President.
</p><p>For fiscal year 2017, Abiomed reported $445.3 million in revenue and reported diluted earnings per share was $1.17 for the year.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>As of March 2019, Michael R. Minogue was Chairman, CEO &amp; President of the company, while Dr. David M. Weber was the Chief Operating Officer. According to Bloomberg, the company "engages in the research, development, and sale of medical devices to assist or replace the pumping function of the failing heart. It also provides continuum of care to heart failure patients". Earlier in the year, the company had increased its 2019 revenue guidance to approximately $780 million.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiomed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Abiomed was founded in Danvers by David M. Lederman<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> in 1981 as Applied Biomedical Corporation, and began working on the development of an <a href="/wiki/Artificial_heart" title="Artificial heart">artificial heart</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Funded by federal research grants,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Lederman partnered with <a href="/wiki/The_Texas_Heart_Institute" title="The Texas Heart Institute">The Texas Heart Institute</a> to develop the <a href="/wiki/AbioCor" title="AbioCor">AbioCor</a>, implanting the first artificial heart into a Kentucky man in July 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  Fourteen of the AbioCor devices were implanted, during clinical trials from 2001 to 2004, with the longest-living recipient surviving 512 days.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The AbioCor won FDA approval in 2006 for patients who are near death and do not qualify for a heart transplant.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2004, Michael R. Minogue became president and CEO of Abiomed.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In 2005, Abiomed purchased <a href="/wiki/Ventricular_assist_device" title="Ventricular assist device">ventricular assist device</a> company Impella CardioSystems AG of Aachen, Germany,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> maker of the <a href="/wiki/Impella" title="Impella">Impella</a> heart pump, developed by Thorsten Siess,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> who is now the Chief Technology Officer at Abiomed.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Minogue helped found the Medical Technology Veterans Program (MVP), a career training and mentorship initiative designed to help veterans entering the civilian workforce transition into jobs in the medical device and life sciences industries.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> He currently serves as chairman of the program. In 2019, Abiomed was announced the 4th best <a href="/wiki/Stock" title="Stock">stock</a> of the 2010s, with total return of 1,983%.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impella_products">Impella products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiomed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Impella products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since 2005 when Abiomed acquired the <a href="/wiki/Impella" title="Impella">Impella</a> technology,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> the latter's heart devices have received a series of FDA approvals.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2007, the Impella 2.5 heart pump was among 35 healthcare products to receive a 2007 Medical Design Excellence Award.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As of March 2019, the Impella heart pump products included the Impella 2.5, Impella 5.0/LD, Impella CP and Impella RP.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> On 28 February 2018, the <a href="/wiki/FDA" class="mw-redirect" title="FDA">FDA</a> approved the concept for the new Impella Ventricular Support Systems - P140003/S018. The system consists of left-side heart pump models (listed above) "intended to help pump blood in patients who need short-term support (up to 6 days)... mounted at the end of a thin, flexible tube (catheter), a console that drives the pump, and an infusion system that flushes the pump". The FDA report added the following specifics about the system: "The Impella Ventricular Support System helps pump blood by drawing blood out of the heart and pumping it into the aorta, partially or fully bypassing the left ventricle. It is implanted into the left side of a patient's heart through a small incision in the femoral artery (major artery in the leg). It can also be implanted through a small incision in a subclavian artery (an artery in the chest)."<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Impella pumps sell for about <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.forbes.com/sites/elliekincaid/2018/12/18/abiomed-sells-600-million-worth-of-its-pencil-sized-heart-pump-but-now-some-doctors-have-big-questions/#6eb69a17753a">$23,000</a>.
</p>
